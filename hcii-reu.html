REU Program

I spent the summer with CMU’s Human-Computer Interaction Institute’s REU program. The research experience for undergraduates (REU) program is an NSF-funded opportunity for undergraduates to get fully immersed in research for a summer. The HCII hosted 34 students from universities across the country (Yale, UT Austin, and UC Berkeley, to name a few) to work closely with faculty advisors on projects ranging from educational technology to privacy and security.

The HCII did an incredible job hosting us. Everything was very well organized, with weekly talks from faculty and PhD students and a plethora of social bonding events. Hats off to the HCII organizers!

McLearn Lab

I worked in the McLearn Lab — advised by Professor Bruce McLaren and with fantastic mentorship from research programmers Leah Teffera and Hayden Stec. The McLearn Lab does research in the fields of learning science, educational technology, digital learning games, and intelligent tutoring systems. Past work includes Decimal Point, a game teaching decimals to middle schoolers, which was shown to be more effective than traditional instruction.

I was one of five interns in the McLearn Lab, all developing learning games or intelligent tutoring systems.

The McLearn Lab interns with Professor Bruce McLaren and research programmer Hayden Stec

By the nature of research in educational technology, the lab functions as a software development team during the summer — writing the technology that will be studied in classrooms throughout the school year. We operated on an agile methodology, with two-week sprints consisting of a feedback loop with stakeholders, code reviews, and daily standups.

The lab encouraged tight collaboration to solve common challenges while also stepping into each other’s projects to provide implementation feedback and suggestions. Final decisions often came from a collaborative debate, where everyone contributed and argued for their perspective.

Future of Work Project

The project I worked on — the Future of Work project — realizes that there has been an explosive demand for workers in the IT sector. While these jobs have traditionally been reserved for workers with four-year university degrees, employers are now considering workers with nontraditional backgrounds as long as they have the right skills.

Seeking to capitalize on this opportunity, we partnered with a local community college. The goal is to use the power of educational technology to prepare students, who often come from disadvantaged backgrounds, for the many opportunities in the digital economy.

My work was centered on designing and developing intelligent tutoring systems (ITS) for introductory IT courses. This did not only include extensive software development, but also research in learning science to effectively target and design tutors. I collaborated with research staff and faculty at CMU to find the most impactful skills to address, and with community college faculty to identify common student pain points. My work also included think-aloud user testing, evaluating both the user and learning experience.

A Cross-Functional Project

The Future of Work team is much bigger than just the McLearn Lab, spanning multiple departments and colleges at CMU. And even beyond Carnegie Mellon with collaborators at the Community College of Allegheny County. The project is only possible because functional teams, with unique sets of expertise, come together to create something that no one team could make on their own.

Because the responsibility of the project is shared among multiple teams, I was provided with great opportunities for cross-functional collaboration. Some of the faculty and teams I had the pleasure of working with were:

  • Carolyn Rose’s team, with expertise in computer-supported collaborative learning (CSCL), to integrate CSCL activities with intelligent tutors;

  • Majd Sakr’s team, teaching computer science and information technology, to tailor our instructional content;

  • Lee Branstetter’s team, experts in economics and public policy, to adapt our work to the broader context and ensure the maximum societal impact;

  • Lauren Herckis, an anthropologist and curriculum design expert, to identify learning objectives and skills to target; and

  • Community college faculty, providing invaluable insights into prerequisite knowledge, student pain points, and expected outcomes.

It was an incredibly exciting project to work on, with a core of software development combined with learning science research and extensive cross-functional collaboration. Click on my research poster to learn more about the project.
